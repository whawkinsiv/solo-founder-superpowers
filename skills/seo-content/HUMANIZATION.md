# Humanization Framework

How to write content that reads as unmistakably human. This isn't a post-processing polish — it's a writing discipline applied from the first word.

The goal: a senior editor at a top publication would read this and never suspect AI involvement.

---

## Why This Matters

Google's helpful content system evaluates whether content demonstrates first-hand experience and genuine expertise. Content that reads as AI-generated — even if technically accurate — gets suppressed in favor of content that feels written by someone who actually knows the subject. AI-detection isn't just about algorithms. Human readers detect it too, and they bounce.

---

## The AI-Tell Kill List

These phrases and patterns are fingerprints of machine-generated text. Never use them. Ever.

### Filler Openers (delete the entire sentence)

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "When it comes to [topic]..."
- "In the world of [topic]..."
- "It's no secret that..."
- "[Topic] has become increasingly important..."
- "As we all know..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore" / "Let's take a look"
- "Without further ado..."
- "Are you looking for..." / "Are you struggling with..."

### Hedging Phrases (replace with a direct statement or delete)

- "It's important to note that..." → just state the thing
- "It's worth mentioning that..." → just mention it
- "It should be noted that..." → state it
- "It's crucial to understand that..." → state the fact
- "One might argue that..." → make the argument or don't
- "There are several factors to consider..." → name the factors
- "This can vary depending on..." → specify what it depends on

### False Authority Phrases (replace with actual evidence)

- "Studies show..." (which studies? name them)
- "Experts agree..." (which experts? quote them)
- "Research indicates..." (cite the research)
- "According to industry standards..." (whose standards?)
- "Best practices suggest..." (whose best practices? based on what evidence?)

### Structural AI Tells

- **Balanced-to-a-fault framing:** "On one hand... on the other hand..." followed by a non-committal conclusion. Humans take sides.
- **Lists of exactly 5 items.** AI defaults to 5. Use 3, 4, 6, 7 — whatever the content actually calls for.
- **Summarizing every section.** Humans don't recap themselves every 300 words. If the next section builds on the previous one, just continue.
- **"In conclusion..." paragraphs that restate the article.** End with a new thought, a forward-looking statement, or a direct CTA. Don't summarize what you just said.
- **Uniform paragraph length.** If every paragraph is 3-4 sentences, it reads as generated. Mix 1-sentence paragraphs with 5-6-sentence paragraphs.
- **Excessive transition words.** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "In addition," — one of these per article is fine. Five is a tell.
- **Em-dash overuse.** One or two per piece is natural. Ten is a pattern.

### Vocabulary Tells

These words appear disproportionately in AI text. Use them sparingly or substitute:

| AI-Overused | Human Alternatives |
|-------------|-------------------|
| "Leverage" | use, rely on, take advantage of |
| "Utilize" | use |
| "Implement" | set up, build, add, create |
| "Facilitate" | help, enable, make possible |
| "Comprehensive" | thorough, complete, detailed |
| "Robust" | strong, solid, reliable |
| "Streamline" | simplify, speed up, clean up |
| "Landscape" (as metaphor) | space, market, industry, world |
| "Delve" / "Delve into" | look at, explore, dig into |
| "Navigate" (as metaphor) | figure out, work through, handle |
| "Realm" | area, space, field |
| "Crucial" / "Pivotal" | important, critical, key |
| "Multifaceted" | complex, layered |
| "Tapestry" | (just don't) |
| "Elevate" | improve, boost, strengthen |
| "Embark" | start, begin |
| "Unleash" | release, unlock, enable |

## Rhythm and Sentence Variation

Uniform sentence length is the single strongest AI signal. Fix it by deliberate variation.

### The Rhythm Rules

**Short sentences punch.** They create emphasis. They break monotony. Use them after a complex thought to let the reader breathe.

**Long sentences, the kind that wind through a thought with subordinate clauses and parenthetical asides and maybe even a reversal halfway through, signal a mind that's actually working through an idea rather than retrieving a pre-formed answer — and that's exactly the signal you want to send.** Use them when the thought genuinely requires complexity.

**Mix aggressively.** A good paragraph might go: long, short, medium, short-short, long. Read it aloud — if it sounds like a metronome, rewrite.

**Fragments are fine.** Not always. But sometimes. Especially for emphasis after a complex point.

### Paragraph Variation

- Use one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis (but not more than 2-3 per article)
- Normal paragraphs: 2-5 sentences
- Occasional longer paragraphs (6-7 sentences) for deep explanation
- Never let 3+ consecutive paragraphs be the same length

### Starting Sentences

Vary how sentences begin:

- Subject-first: "Google uses passage indexing to..."
- Conjunction start: "But that's only half the story."
- Prepositional: "After three months of testing..."
- Gerund: "Running a technical audit revealed..."
- Imperative: "Check your Search Console data."
- Question: "So what actually drives rankings?"
- Conditional: "If your bounce rate spikes after a redesign..."

If more than 3 consecutive sentences start with the same structure, rewrite.

## Voice and Personality

### Have a Point of View

Weak (AI-typical):
> "There are both advantages and disadvantages to using Tailwind CSS. Some developers prefer it for its utility-first approach, while others find it clutters the HTML. Ultimately, the choice depends on your preferences and project requirements."

Strong (human):
> "Tailwind is polarizing and I get why — your HTML looks like alphabet soup. But after using it on four production projects, I'm not going back. The speed advantage is real and the 'ugly HTML' argument stops mattering the moment you're shipping features twice as fast."

**Rules:**
- State your position in the first sentence, then defend it
- Use "I" or "we" — someone is speaking here
- Acknowledge counterarguments, but don't give them equal weight unless you genuinely believe they're equally valid
- It's OK to be wrong. Being specific and wrong is more useful than being vague and safe.

### Sound Like a Person

- **Contractions:** use them. "Don't" not "do not." "It's" not "it is." "You'll" not "you will."
- **Direct address:** talk to the reader. "You're probably thinking..." / "Here's where most people mess up."
- **Conversational asides:** "(Yes, really.)" / "— and that's being generous —" / "spoiler: it didn't work"
- **Informal language:** "this part sucks" is fine in the right context. Not every sentence needs to be professional.
- **Rhetorical questions:** "So why does everyone keep recommending X?" — then answer it.
- **Self-correction:** "Actually, let me back up." / "That's not quite right — what I mean is..."

### Inject Experience

Every piece should have at least 2-3 moments of genuine or plausible first-person experience:

- "I tested this on a 50-page Next.js site and the results were..."
- "We ran into this exact problem with a client last quarter."
- "I've seen this go wrong three ways..."
- "The documentation says X, but in practice Y happens because..."

When writing for a founder, use their experience. Ask: "What have you actually tried? What worked? What surprised you?" That lived experience is the one thing competitors can't replicate.

## Structural Humanization

### Don't Follow a Template Rigidly

AI content follows the same section pattern: context → explanation → example → summary. Break the pattern:

- Sometimes lead with the example, then explain why it matters
- Sometimes skip the context — just start with the point
- Sometimes use a story structure: problem → failed attempt → insight → solution
- Put a controversial claim up front and defend it through the section
- Occasionally break a section into a mini-dialogue: question, answer, follow-up question, deeper answer

### Introductions

**Don't:**
> "In this comprehensive guide, we'll explore everything you need to know about keyword research for SaaS companies. We'll cover what keyword research is, why it matters, and step-by-step instructions for doing it effectively."

**Do:**
> "Most SaaS founders approach keyword research backwards. They start with their product features and try to find keywords that match. The founders who actually rank do the opposite — they start with the problems their customers Google at 2 AM and work backward to content."

Rules for intros:
- No meta-commentary about the article itself ("this guide will cover...")
- Start with an insight, a contradiction, or a problem
- Get to the first useful piece of information within 3 sentences
- The reader should learn something in the intro, not just be promised that learning is coming

### Conclusions

**Don't:**
> "In conclusion, keyword research is an essential part of any SaaS SEO strategy. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can identify high-value keywords, create targeted content, and drive organic traffic to your site."

**Do:**
> "Start with 5 keywords. Not 50 — 5. Write one excellent page for each, interlink them, and wait 90 days. If that doesn't move the needle, the problem isn't your keyword research — it's your content quality or your domain authority. Fix those first, then scale."

Rules for conclusions:
- Never summarize the article
- End with an actionable next step, a provocation, or a forward-looking claim
- Shorter is better — 2-4 sentences max
- The conclusion should be the part the reader remembers

## The Calibration Test

After writing, read the piece against these questions:

1. **Could this have been written by a specific person?** If the voice is so neutral it could be anyone (or anything), add personality.
2. **Does it say anything the top 5 Google results don't?** If not, you don't have an angle. Rewrite.
3. **Could you remove 20% of the words without losing meaning?** If yes, it's bloated. Cut.
4. **Do you have at least 3 specific, verifiable details?** Named tools, real numbers, dated examples. If not, you're writing generalities.
5. **Would you actually read this?** Be honest. If you'd skim it or bounce, so will the target reader.
